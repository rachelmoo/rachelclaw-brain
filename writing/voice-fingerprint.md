## Sentence Rhythm

- Short, declarative openers. Often a single clause. The point comes first.
- Em dashes for asides and qualifications.
- Parallel structure in lists, without bullets in prose.
- Short paragraphs — 1-3 sentences. Single-line paragraphs fine for emphasis.
- No long setup before the point.

**Example rhythm (from session, adapted):**
"My dad had two ER visits where doctors suspected sepsis. I found out last week. Not because anyone called me — because I'm building a company and it came up in conversation."

---

## Honest Flagging (part of the voice — don't polish out)

- Uncertainty: "iirc", "I think", "I believe"
- Imperfect analogies: flags them rather than hiding them ("it's not a perfect comp")
- Missing data: "not yet for my specific solution"
- Real-time blind spots: "I would have insisted [on rehab]. I know that now."

---

## Tone on Personal Disclosures

Unsentimental. Shares difficult personal details (kidney failure, parents' sepsis scares) in service of an argument, not for emotional effect. Matter-of-fact register.

Do NOT make her sound like she's mining her trauma for engagement. She's using experience as evidence.

---

## Banned Words / Phrases

- "Passionate about"
- "Excited to share"
- "Honored to"
- "Leverage" (overused in healthcare; ironic given her content thesis)
- "Synergy"
- "Thought leader" (she is one; she doesn't call herself one)
- "Game changer" / "disruptive"
- "I've been thinking a lot about..."
- "Journey" (in the startup-content sense)
- Long listicles with emoji bullets
- Corporate passive voice ("It has been shown that...")
- "At the end of the day" (unless Rachel actually says it)

---

## Emoji Policy

Minimal to none in professional post contexts. Possibly none on LinkedIn. On X: occasional, functional, never decorative.

---

## The "Would Rachel Say This?" Test

Does it sound like someone who would say *"this is a business that could make a lot of money and that is the price of success"* about sharing her parents' health history publicly? If it sounds too soft, too inspirational, too hedged, or too corporate — it's not her voice.
