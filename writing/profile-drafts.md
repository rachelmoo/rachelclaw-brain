## LinkedIn About (draft 1)

> The first time I called 911, I was visiting my parents in South Carolina. My dad was on a business trip. Just me and my mom when her heart episode started.
>
> That was years ago. Since then, my mom has had cancer multiple times, a TIA, and ongoing cardiac issues. My dad had an emergency triple bypass. They're both still independent, still sharp — but they end up in the hospital at least once a year. And most of the time, I'm 2,500 miles away in San Francisco, finding out what happened days, weeks, or sometimes years later.
>
> Last week I found out my dad had two separate ER visits where doctors suspected sepsis. I found out because I'm building a company and it came up in conversation. Not because anyone called me.
>
> After his bypass, my dad refused rehab. He didn't trust anyone to give him an accurate cost estimate. I didn't know enough at the time to push back. I know now what that probably cost him in his recovery.
>
> I also navigate this system as a patient myself — one kidney removed, the other slowly degrading, and a few things still unexplained. Even with a tech background and a high tolerance for complexity, it's genuinely hard.
>
> When I started building Sagebeam and looked at what tools existed for family caregivers, I found almost nothing.
>
> Meanwhile: 59 million Americans are caring for a loved one with two or more chronic conditions. 10,000 people turn 65 every day. Most family caregivers are women — many quit their careers, give up their economic independence, spend their last healthy years caring for someone else before they get sick themselves.
>
> And the system keeps pushing more onto them. Hospital at home. Shorter lengths of stay. Reduced follow-up. With no support, no tools, no information — not just clinically, but legally, financially, emotionally, logistically.
>
> There's a war on caregivers in this country that nobody is talking about.
>
> LLMs finally make it possible to build what these caregivers actually need: something that meets them where they are, handles real complexity, and doesn't require a care manager to manage it. The research is clear — when you support the family caregiver, outcomes improve and costs come down. The problem was always the delivery mechanism.
>
> We're fixing that.
>
> If you work in health systems, MA plans, specialty groups, or post-acute care — I'd love to connect. This problem lives in your world too.

## X Bio options

**Option A — personal/provocative:**
> There's a war on caregivers in America. Nobody's talking about it. Building @Sagebeam to change that. Long-distance daughter. Chronic illness patient.

**Option B — thesis-driven (investor/founder crowd):**
> Family caregivers are subsidizing the US healthcare system with their health and their careers. LLMs finally let us fix it. Building @Sagebeam.

A lands harder emotionally; B is sharper for a scrolling VC. Could combine: A as hook, B as logic.
